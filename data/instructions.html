1. About

This is a Sudoku puzzle generator and solver. See the Wikipedia tab or go to http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudoku for general info about Sudoku puzzles and strategies for solving them.

 

2. Instructions

 

2.1 Entering Numbers

Click on a cell, then press a number key on the keyboard. Hit delete, backspace, or space to clear a cell. You can also clear a cell by pressing the number that it currently contains.

 

2.2 Entering Markups

To enter a markup, right click a cell and enter digits from 1 to 9. Pressing a digit once will enter amarkup. Pressing the same digit again will clear it. To clear all markups, hit delete or backspace.

For information on the uses of markups, see Wikipedia.

 

2.3 The Lock Checkbox

Locked cells are highlighted in blue. You cannot enter text in a locked cell. Unchecking lock will unlock all cells. Checking lock will lock down all filled cells, and check the puzzle for difficulty and solvability. You can use this feature to create your own puzzles or enter a puzzle from another source.

 

2.4 The Conflicts Checkbox

If conflicts is checked, the program will highlight conflicts in red as you enter them. Note that just because a cell is not highlighted does not mean it is correct, only that there is no obvious conflict right now.

 

2.5 The New Button

Creates a new puzzle. You will be asked to choose a difficulty, and then a new Sudoku puzzle of that difficulty will be created for you.

 

2.6 Puzzle Difficulty

Very Easy, Easy and Moderate: These puzzles can be solved with a scanning strategy (see Wikipedia).

Hard and Very Hard: These puzzles will require a markup/analysis strategy (see Wikipedia) at some point.

Extremely Hard: These puzzles will require a trial and error strategy at some point. Schmudoku will not generate Extremely Hard puzzles for you, but it can recognize an Extremely Hard puzzle if you enter one and lock it in.

 

2.7 The Solve Button

Automatically solves the puzzle based on the locked cells. Cells that the computer fills in during this process will be highlighted in yellow, and cells that you had filled incorrectly will be highlighted in red.

 

2.8 The Clear Button

Clears unlocked cells and/or annotations. You will be asked to choose whether you want to clear all the main entries, the markups, just the conflicts, or everything.

 

2.9 The Undo and Redo Buttons

The < and > buttons are for undoing and redoing operations. These buttons are enabled only when undo and/or redo are available. The undo buffer contains 100 entries.

 

3. How Schumdoku! Works

 

3.1 Solving and Rating Puzzles

Schmudoku! rates a puzzle by attempting to solve it. The first attempt uses only scanning (see Wikipedia) -- Schmudoku! scans rows, columns and 3x3 boxes looking for a digit that can only go in one spot. If the puzzle can be solved this way, then it is rated as either Very Easy, Easy, or Moderate. The particular rating depends mostly on the average number of cells that were filled in on each complete pass (where a pass is a complete scan of every row, column, and box), but it also takes into account the number of bottlenecks encountered. A bottleneck is any complete scan that results in only one new cell being filled in.

If the puzzle cannot be solved by scanning, another attempt is made using both scanning and markup strategies. Hard puzzles are those that can be solved by a combination of scanning and a markup strategy that looks for cells that can only be filled in by a single digit. Very Hard puzzles also involve the use of a markup strategy that engages in candidate elimination by identifying matched cell groups (see Wikipedia).

If the puzzle cannot be solved by scanning or marking up, Schmudoku! resorts to a backtracking algorithm. The backtracking algorithm tries all possible combinations of digits in the remaining cells looking for one combination that works. If the puzzle can only be solved this way, then it is classified as Extremely Hard. If not, then it is unsolveable. Backtracking can alsoidentify whether the puzzle has more than one solution.

 

3.2 Creating Puzzles

Schmudoku! creates puzzles by first finding a valid solved puzzle (there are very few of these relative to the total number of 9x9 arrangements of digits), and then blanking out cells a few at a time and attempting to solve the resulting puzzles.

Schmudoku! finds valid solved puzzles using a technique called Random Mutation Hill Climbing. It starts by arranging the digits 1 to 9 randomly in each 3x3 box. Then it produces a score for the puzzle by counting the number of conflicts in each row and column (the number of repeated digits). A score of 0 is a valid solved puzzle, anything higher must be changed. At each step of the hill climbing, Schmudoku! randomly chooses one of the 3x3 boxes and then randomly swaps two of the digits within that box. Then it scores the board again. If the board gets a better (lower) score, it is kept. If it doesn't, it gets thrown away. The process continues until a valid solved puzzle is found. (It's like trying to climb a hill by taking one step at a time in a random direction, and then figuring out whether you are higher or lower than you were before.)

The next step is to create a partially filled in puzzle. Schmudoku! does this by randomly blanking out a few cells at a time and attempting to solve the puzzle. If the puzzle cannot be solved using scanning and markup strategies, the blanked out cells are filled back in and the process repeats. After Schmudoku! can make no more progress in this way, it is finished and the puzzle can be rated. You may have noticed that Schmudoku! produces symmetrical boards. This is because it always blanks out 2 or 4 cells at a time, and does it in such a way as to keep the symmetry of the board.

Not all puzzles created in this way are acceptable. If the puzzle has more than 30 clues, or does not fit any of the rating criteria (i.e., it doesn't fit the definition of Very Easy, Easy, Moderate, Hard, or Very Hard), then it is discarded and the whole process continues until a puzzle is found that fits the criteria. That's why it can sometimes take a few seconds to find a new puzzle.

 

3.2 Inefficiencies

There are lots of inefficiencies in the board creation process described above, and it's an ongoing project to produce a more efficient board creator. The first main focus will be on the process that goes from a solved puzzle to a blanked-out one. This algorithm should really check the difficulty level as it goes and throw away puzzles that don't fit the current level of difficulty. But this is harder than it sounds because the difficulty ratings don't make much sense until most of the cells are blank. (To see this, try solving a board using Schmudoku, then blank out one cell, and unlokc and lock to generate a rating). But if this step can be improved, then fewer puzzles will be produced that don't fit the criteria, and the puzzle creation process will be considerably faster.
